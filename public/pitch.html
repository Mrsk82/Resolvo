CX Strategy
A CX (Customer Experience) strategy is a plan for how every interaction a customer has with your brand — from first email to final resolution — should feel and function. Without one, support is reactive: you firefight tickets instead of preventing them. A good CX strategy defines response time targets, escalation rules, self-service options, and feedback loops so your team always knows what "good" looks like and customers always know what to expect.
The three levers are self-service, deflection, and proactive communication. Self-service means a Knowledge Base customers search before emailing you. Deflection means a widget that suggests relevant articles before the customer hits "Submit". Proactive communication means emailing customers about a known issue before they notice it — which turns a complaint into goodwill. Teams using all three typically see 40-60% fewer tickets with the same or higher CSAT scores.
The core CX metrics are: FRT (First Response Time — how fast you acknowledge a ticket), TTR (Time to Resolution — how fast you fully solve it), SLA Breach Rate (% of tickets that miss their deadline), CSAT (customer satisfaction score after resolution), and Reopen Rate (how often a "resolved" ticket comes back — signals bad fixes). Track these weekly, not monthly — problems compound fast in support.
Speed and ownership are everything. Acknowledge within 15 minutes, assign an owner, and give the customer a timeline — even if that timeline is "we'll update you in 2 hours." Never pass a customer between agents without a warm handoff. The customer should never have to re-explain their issue. Internally, escalations should trigger a separate SLA clock with tighter targets and alert the team lead automatically.
Reactive support waits for the customer to raise a problem. Proactive CX anticipates it. Examples: send a status email before the customer notices an outage, follow up 3 days after resolution to confirm everything is still working, warn customers about an upcoming change before it happens. Proactive CX costs a few minutes per incident and saves hours of inbound ticket volume — and customers remember it far longer than any quick fix.
The key is shared visibility. When engineers can see which bugs are causing the most customer tickets, support priorities become product priorities. Weekly stand-ups where support shares the top 5 recurring ticket reasons with engineering create natural alignment. Showing the revenue impact of bugs — not just ticket count — makes CX a business conversation, not just a support conversation. Tools like Resolvo connect these two worlds in one platform.
Start with your actual data, not industry benchmarks. Look at your last 90 days: what was your median FRT and TTR? Set your SLA targets 20% tighter than your median — achievable but stretching. Segment by priority: Critical tickets should have a 4h target, routine queries 24-48h. Review and tighten every quarter as your team improves. Importantly, publish your SLA commitments to customers — accountability improves performance.
Tag every ticket with a reason code (e.g. "billing confusion", "feature missing", "bug", "onboarding"). Review the top 5 tags monthly in a joint support + product session. Each tag cluster is a product signal. Low CSAT scores on a specific feature point directly to where UX needs work. When you close the loop — fixing the root cause and emailing affected customers to say "you told us this was hard, we fixed it" — you convert detractors into advocates.
FCR (First Contact Resolution) is the % of tickets fully resolved without needing a follow-up from the customer. Industry average is around 70-75%; great teams hit 85%+. To improve it: build a comprehensive internal knowledge base so agents don't guess, give agents authority to resolve common issues without manager sign-off, and analyse reopened tickets every week to find repeat failure patterns. Each reopened ticket is a signal of an incomplete first answer.
The fastest path is shadowing + guided workload ramp. Week 1: new agent reads every resolved ticket from the past month, no independent handling. Week 2: handles tickets with a senior agent reviewing before send. Week 3: independent with daily review. Use canned response templates for common queries so new agents get the tone right from day one. Track their FRT and CSAT separately for the first 60 days — it tells you exactly where training gaps are.
Follow this sequence: 1) Acknowledge publicly within 15 minutes (even if you don't know the cause yet — "We're aware and investigating"), 2) Update every 30 minutes with progress, 3) Send a post-mortem email within 24 hours explaining what happened, what you fixed, and what prevents it happening again. Customers forgive outages. They don't forgive silence. A transparent, proactive response to an outage often increases trust more than months of smooth operation.
Automate the routine, humanise the critical. Automation is excellent for: acknowledgement emails, status updates, FAQ deflection, ticket routing, SLA reminders, and follow-up surveys. Human touch is irreplaceable for: billing disputes, enterprise complaints, anything involving frustration or urgency, and any moment where the customer feels they haven't been heard. The mistake most teams make is automating apologies — a canned "we're sorry" after a serious issue makes things worse, not better.
